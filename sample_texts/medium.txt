Information hiding is a broad discipline that encompasses both cryptography and steganography.
While cryptography focuses on making information unreadable to unauthorized parties, steganography
goes further by concealing the very existence of a secret message. Together, these two techniques
form a powerful framework for secure and covert digital communication in modern networks.

The history of steganography stretches back thousands of years. Ancient Greeks shaved the heads
of messengers, tattooed secret messages on their scalps, and waited for the hair to grow back
before sending them on their way. During World War II, invisible ink and microdots were used
extensively to pass intelligence between agents. Today, digital steganography has replaced these
physical methods with sophisticated computational techniques that can hide data in virtually any
type of digital media.

Unicode-based text steganography is a particularly elegant approach to information hiding. Modern
text encoding standards support hundreds of thousands of distinct characters, many of which are
visually indistinguishable from ordinary whitespace. By substituting regular space characters
with these visually identical Unicode alternatives, it is possible to embed binary data directly
within plain text documents without altering their visible appearance in any detectable way.

The capacity of a text steganography system depends on the number of available embedding slots
and the number of bits encoded per slot. A system using four distinct Unicode space characters
encodes two bits per space, while a system with sixteen characters can encode four bits per
space. This relationship means that expanding the alphabet can significantly increase the amount
of secret data that can be concealed within a given cover text, making it a valuable parameter
for optimizing steganographic capacity.

Detection of Unicode-based steganography requires specialized analysis tools capable of examining
the byte-level representation of text files. Standard text editors and word processors typically
render all whitespace characters identically, making visual inspection alone insufficient for
detection. Forensic analysis must therefore operate at the Unicode code point level, comparing
the actual character values used for spacing against the expected distribution of normal text.
This requirement for specialized tools provides a meaningful layer of security for legitimate
covert communication applications.
